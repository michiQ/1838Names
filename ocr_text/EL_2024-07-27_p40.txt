           Also pertinent to my analysis here is chapter two of Chaotic Justice entitled,

“Truth Stranger than Fiction: African American Identity and (Auto)Biography.” In this

chapter, Ernest asks, “How did the chaotic constructions of race shape individual lives,

and what are the implications of these constructions for writers and readers of African

American autobiographies and biographies?” To answer this question, Ernest points to

“crafted silences,” and “strategic omissions” in African American (auto)biography that

point to, “stories beyond what white audiences were prepared to hear or capable of

understanding.”49 This certainly applies to (auto)biographical writing about Edmonia

Lewis. As a young Indigenous and African American woman who did not fit neatly

into the social roles assigned to nonwhite women at her time, Lewis would have to

clear several “hurdles,” to borrow Ernest’s phrasing. Indeed, these “hurdles led many

writers not only to novelize their narratives but also to fictionalize their lives and their

worlds so as to get at the realities of a world defined by race that was barely

apprehended by white writers and readers.”50 After reading and re-reading How

Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist, and looking at the realities that shaped her world, I

believe that she, like the other Black women engaged with (auto)biographical writing,
intentionally fictionalized her life. While I understand why some scholars use words
