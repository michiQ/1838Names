activists and intellectuals who created momentum for the artists and thinkers who

came of age at the turn of the century. Currently, however, there is only a small corpus

of scholarship on Lewis, and it exists almost exclusively within the framework of art

history. While productive for understanding the formal qualities of her sculptures, it is

through the lens of African American Studies that we can see the rich complexity of

her life as a traveler and interlocutor in the Black Atlantic cultural world of the

nineteenth century long before such travels were made famous by the New Negro

expatriates of the twentieth century. Of Lewis’ personal correspondence, only a few

letters remain, and these letters are found in the archives of other historical figures,

such as that of her temporary patron, Lydia Maria Child, or in the archive of

nineteenth century newspapers. This seemingly limited personal archive has led to a

rather narrow reading of her life in scholarly discourse: her sculpture is the subject of

art historical treatment, but her transatlantic life and her place in a broader history of

African American culture and politics has been difficult to reconstruct. In the field of

art history, Lewis often seems to stand alone and apart as a Black woman creator of
