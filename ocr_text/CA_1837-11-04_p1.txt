Tue following well-written account of the so”

an irremediable consumption is fast pushing him,
it will have weight with all who are willing to
know the truth.

Who has not heard it? Whoahat has
read the New York Courier, or its neighbor
the Commercial, or the African Repository.
or the Boston Recorder, or its echo prints in
Maine, New Hampshire andVermont, or the
thousand and one other pro-slavery papers,
from Duff Green’s Telegraph and the Wash-
ington Globe, down to Paul Pry and the
New Hampshire Patriot, have not been hori-
fied with the pictures of tie idleness, poverty,
misery, insubordination, crime, and destruc-
tion which those sage papers asserted would
attend and have attended emancipation in the
British West Indies 2? Who has not heard
of the laziness and ingratitude of the appren-
tices and freemen ? of the falling off of the
crops ? of the depreciation of property ? and
of the stagnation of trade ? in short, as one
of our Southern governors said in one of his
messages, the consummated ruin of all those
isltnds to which British ‘fanaticism’ com-
pelled British legislation to give impartially,
to all colors, British freedom and British
rights! Well, we wish all such persons

everywhere be around them, and walk before
them, and speak to then: in language not to
be mistaken or perverted, and which would
stamp on their minds such images of emanci-
pation as they and their teachers, in their

ed of. They would be astonished and ama-
zed, as well as interested and richly instructed
by the very odd and unnataral forms which

rection have taken to themselves in these

A writer of the N. Y. American furnishes
the following affecting incident concerning
the loss of an accomplished family, with the
Steam-packet Home :—

“In the event of a calamity like that of
the steam ship Home, the friends of the un-
fortunate sufferers are apt to consider those
for whom they are immediately interested,
objects of interest to all others. The writer
of this sketch is one of those who think so ;
and if there be any case more touching than
that detailed below, it must be heart-rending
indeed !

“I became acquainted with Mr. H. Croom,
Esq., somewhat more than a year ago, while
he was spending the summer at the North,
partially for the benefit of his health, and to
visit his children, who were at school in this
city. Mr. Croém was educated for the North
Carolina bar, but possessing an ample for-
tune, and a general taste for literature, he
cultivated his plantation near Newbern, to-
gether with his scientific pursuits, especially
that of botany, of which he was particularly
fond, and upon which he has published seve-
ral works, solely with the view of extending
the science. If there was ever a modest, un-
pretending man, it was he; with a mind of
great capacity, and with attainments rarely
excelled, he had the modesty of a woman,
and the simplicity of a child. The evening
prior to his departure on board that ill-fated
ship, I spent with him and shis family, con-
sisting of his wife, his wife’s aunt, and his
three children, the eldest of whom, a daugh-
ter, had just completed her education at Ma-
dame Chegary’s school. Any one that has
seen that lovely girl will know how impossi-
ble it is to describe her. She was indeed
beautiful: her mind was of superior order,
and her accomplishments were equalled by
few, and surpassed by none of her age, whic
was not far from sixteen. The second was
an interesting boy, of about twelve; the
third was a daughter, yet a child. During
the evening, they were speaking of their in-
tended voyage with pleasure, and anticipating
an early return next summer, Miss Croom,
all animation and loveliness, her hair braided
aud festooned after the Swiss fashion, as she
playfully moved through the well-lighted
apartments, appeared like a being that exists
only in romance, more than one who has an
abode in this ever changing’ planet. Mr. C.
was describing the formation of a favorite
plant that he had found in the recess of the
everglades of Florida,and giving it its proper
botanic name. All was joy and gaiety in
forming plans of pleasure for their return to
the North the next season, and during their
sojourn in Charleston the coming winter.—
How little did any one of that happy group
think that in less than three days from that
hour, the accomplished Croom, and that at-
tached and united family, would be corpses,
lashed by the billows of a raging ocean !—
Yet such was the case; their hopes, their
fears, their joys have ceased. The howling
storm wailed their destiny—the old and the
young, the beautiful and the gay,have ceased
to live. How truly does this awful calamity
verify the maxim—“ Sola salus, servire
Deo!” J. M. B.”
