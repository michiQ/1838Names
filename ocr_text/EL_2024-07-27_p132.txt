Black people like Lewis who had the resources and desire to travel in comfortable

accommodations, posed a threat to the racial and social order. The Civil Rights Act of

1875 was poorly enforced, and in the post-Reconstruction era, travel segregation

worsened. On American ships, accommodations were segregated. Black passengers

could not take their meals until after the white passengers had eaten, and as on trains,

usually were not provided with comfortable rooms. Improvements in accommodation,

both on public conveyance and in lodging, improved the safety of lone white women

travelers. However, Black women were denied such conveniences and protections, and

faced the unique dangers of potential assault and bodily harm. For this reason, long

distance travel could be exhausting, physically, mentally, and emotionally. According

to news reports, Lewis was denied lodging, physically assaulted, and turned into a

spectacle while traveling in America. And it is possible that she endured more insults

than were reported. Lewis had to navigate racist environments throughout her life. In

Oberlin, a town noted for its progressive race politics, she faced multiple accusations,

of which she was acquitted, and a near-deadly assault. When Lewis visited Chicago in

1870 to exhibit “Hagar in the Wilderness,” she was assaulted again, this time by a man

who did not believe that Hagar was the work of an African and Native American

person.254 While these experiences are not directly related to travel, they point to the
fact that Lewis racial violence and would have been aware of the dangers of traveling

as a Black woman. Lewis persisted in the face of opposition, insisting on creating a

place for herself. Perhaps she knew that she would not have to work as hard to gain an

audience from fellow persons of color.
