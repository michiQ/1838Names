       In the blithe and pleasant Spring-time . . .88

In this excerpt, Hiawatha is characterized as the noble savage: the Indigenous person

with strong ethics who is deeply connected to their environment. Hiawatha’s

characterization closely parallels the description of Lewis’ early life in that hunting,

fishing, and making--connecting with nature--were observed to be central activities of

her life as an Indigenous person. By characterizing herself in this way, Lewis created a

mystique around herself that made her appeal to the many readers who enjoyed

Longfellow’s works.

       Lewis dedicated considerable time and energy creating artistic responses to

Henry Wadsworth Longfellow’s Song of Hiawatha, a best-selling epic poem published

in 1855.89 Within two years, Hiawatha had sold 50,000 copies in the United States

alone. In her 1984 article, “Artistic Interpretations of Henry Wadsworth Longfellow's

‘The Song of Hiawatha,’1855-1900,” art historian Cynthia D. Nickerson calls the

years 1865-1875 a “golden age in Hiawatha interpretations.” During this ten-year

period, Lewis met Longfellow and sculpted a bust of him, and she created a series of

Hiawatha works: “Hiawatha,” “Minnehaha,” “Hiawatha’s Marriage,” and “The Old

Arrow Maker.” Nickerson conjectures that this gap between the publication of the

Hiawatha and its artistic interpretations might have to do with the fact that this epic
poem had an appeal to artists who read it as children, one of whom was Edmonia
