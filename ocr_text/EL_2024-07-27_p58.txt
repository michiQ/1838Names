             An Ever-Present Absence: Lewis’ Parentage and Identity

       The subject of Lewis’ racial identity and parentage is almost always the initial

focus of her interviews. At times, this interest came out of a genuine curiosity and

well-intentioned support. Usually, Lewis addressed the limitations created by the

public’s preoccupation with her race by drawing her reader’s attention to her Native

identity and upbringing. In the first paragraph of How Edmonia Lewis Became an

Artist she addresses her parent’s absence as follows:

       When little Edmonia was only three years old her mother died, leaving
       her to the care of her Indian relatives; and at the grave of her dear
       mother she kissed her father good-by, and has never seen him since.
       Her father died a year from the time of his wife's death. Thus in one
       day she buried both father and mother from her sight.94

I would like to pair this excerpt with some information Lewis provided in interviews.

her parents died when she was about four years old. She was old enough to have

memories of her parents, but not old enough to make detailed assessments of her

parents’ character and personality. Whatever knowledge Lewis had of her parents’
