statement: “Proud as we feel at every great achievement of any woman, we always

feel doubly so, when one of this oppressed race, by her rare gifts, commands a place

among the favored few of earth's children, and proves that genius has no sex or

color.”35 This sentiment could have inspired the pamphlet’s last sentence, “. . . these

two combined enable her to rise above all prejudices of race or color, and command

the respect and honor of all true lovers of art.”36 In November of 1869, The Boston

Christian Register published a similar article, which was reprinted in the National

Anti-Slavery Standard.37 Read together, these articles show that it is possible that How

Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist was pulled from both interviews and articles written

about her. What we can assert with complete confidence is that Edmonia Lewis

created an identity that she used consistently throughout her career, one that makes a

case for her existence as native, and black, and woman, and genius and artist, (not to

mention possibly queer, or at least disrupting gender norms) those identities not being

mutually exclusive. Indeed, her multiple identities were perplexing, not only to her

public, but it seems that she herself was navigating the false logics of racism as she

built her public persona.
