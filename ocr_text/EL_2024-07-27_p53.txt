       Well, one reason is that I love to travel. I have Indian blood in me, you
       know, and I love the grand scenery of the mountains. Why, do you
       know I almost envied the freedom of the Indians which I saw on the
       plains!84

In this case, she did not need to mention her background to answer the interviewer’s

question. She reiterated the connection between Native Americans and their

environment, eliciting scenes that would be familiar to her western readers, and

drawing herself out as unique, and a part of a people viewed as idyllic.

       Such references to these idyllic scenes of Native American life began with the

first paragraph of How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist: “With the Indians she lived

a happy life--which was indeed a wild, roving one--hunting, fishing, and making

moccasins.” And, later in the narrative, Lewis describes herself as Chippewa. Henry

Rowe Schoolcraft popularized the language, practices, and folktales of Ojibwe-

Chippewa cultures. A geographer, geologist and ethnologist, Schoolcraft’s texts

deeply influenced popular understandings of Ojibwe-Chippewa peoples.85

Schoolcraft’s work directly influenced Henry Wadsworth Longfellow’s literary works.
