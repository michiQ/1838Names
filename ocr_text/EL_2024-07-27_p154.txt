her works and Black women who hosted her when she was touring and exhibiting her

       Lewis encountered many of the men listed in Brown’s text throughout her

lifetime. Charles Reason taught math at New York Central College shortly before she

was a student there. Frederick Douglass was her friend and advocate throughout her

adulthood. John M. Langston was her lawyer and loyal supporter. Edward M.

Bannister worked in the same studio building as Lewis when she lived in Boston. This

aspiring class of African Americans—to borrow Michele Mitchell’s term—formed a

social network consisting of friends, colleagues, and sometimes, rivals.298 The

representative men and women of the post-bellum, Pre-Harlem period were

renaissance folk; individuals who cultivated all their talents to contribute to the

establishment of Black cultural, educational, and social institutions and practices. The

story of Black cultural production in post-Bellum, pre-Harlem America could start

with any one of these individuals, and through their story, it would still be possible to

learn about the others.
