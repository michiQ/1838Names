       While a student at Oberlin, Lewis lived in the home of Reverend John Keep, a

trustee of the college and one of Douglass’ many contacts. Keep and his wife Lydia

Hale hosted twelve young ladies in their home, including Lewis. In early 1862, two of

her housemates accused Lewis of poisoning them, after which vigilantes brutally

assaulted her. Lewis’ lawyer, John Mercer Langston, was almost shot while trying to

gather evidence for her defense, yet he won her case. It seemed that there were

individuals who had a vendetta against Lewis. In February of 1863, an art instructor

accused her of stealing art supplies. Even though the prosecutors did not present

enough evidence to support this accusation, the principal of the lady’s department,

Marianne Dascomb, barred Lewis from registering for her last semester. It is

reasonable to assume that these circumstances were a large part of the reason she met

with Douglass. Keep or Langston, both colleagues of Douglass, might have seen it fit

to seek out Douglass’ help in raising the spirits of a student of color, whose parents

had passed on long ago and whose brother was far away. Although Lewis would not

be able to complete her degree, with the help of friends in high places, it would have

been possible for her to completely re-envision and reinvent her life.
       Skipping details around what transpired at Oberlin, he focused on the role he

played in encouraging her to become an artist:

       She had exhibited some signs of talent in drawing and painting; had
       evinced such enthusiasm for the art which adorns and ennobles us that,
       from a kindred artistic love, we were led to advise her to seek the East,
       and by study prepare herself for work and further study abroad.
