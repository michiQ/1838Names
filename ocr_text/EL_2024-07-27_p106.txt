        Lewis benefitted from the political and literary community that was forged in

Boston, Massachusetts in the 1830s. For the most part, the public was, at worst, in

support of the enslavement of African Americans, and at best, apathetic towards the

institution. It was due to the effort of a few, the most active of whom was William

Lloyd Garrison, that the Anti-Slavery movement really began to take shape. This

collective of leaders, made up of white and black professionals, believed slavery to be

a blight on what was supposed to be a republican country where in all people are

created equal. They utilized public forms of literature and literary practice, including

newspapers, tracts, pamphlets, to advance their cause, which led to increased

circulation of information and political activism. It was in this environment that Lewis

was introduced to print culture and began to use it to advance her own career.

message of immediate emancipation across the northeast, with little response, save in

Massachusetts. Zorn, who wrote this history of the New England Anti-Slavery

Society, describes Garrison’s early career in his article, “The New England Anti-
