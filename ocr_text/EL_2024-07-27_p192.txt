       Other Black women, like Gertrude Emily Hicks Bustill Mossell, would

commemorate Edmonia Lewis in text. In 1894, she published The Work of the Afro-

American Women under her married name, Mrs. N.F Mossell. Benjamin F. Lee,

bishop of the A.M.E. Church, wrote the intro on to her text, commending Black

women for their literary attainments. Again, underscoring the A.M.E. Church’s

involvement with Black scholarship. Mrs. Mossell has a chapter entitled “Our Afro-

American Representatives at the World’s Fair.” She starts by expressing

disappointment that African Americans were not represented on the national

committee for the World’s Fair, then transitions into a discussion on African American

representation on the State Boards. She notes that the planning staff Joan Imogene

Howard a “position on the Board of Woman Managers of the State of New York for

the Columbian Exposition,” as “one of five of the Committee on Education” then

follows with a bit of biographical information. She mentions Lewis briefly mentioned

in the first chapter, entitled “The Work of Afro-American Women.” Mossell wrote

that Lewis’ story is so “well known that it scarce needs repetition; her ‘Cleopatra

Dying,’ exhibited at the Centennial Exhibition, received a medal of honor. Most of her

works have been sold to titled persons of Europe.”356 While the mention was brief, it

was significant. Receiving a medal of honor at a national event is not inconsequential,
and it is meaningful that Black woman writer recognized such an accomplishment.
