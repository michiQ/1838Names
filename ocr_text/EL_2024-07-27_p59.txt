       In 1866, a few years before Lewis’ pamphlet was first printed, Henry Wreford

wrote an article about her called “A Negro Sculptress” for the London Athenaeum that

circulated widely throughout the United Kingdom and United States. In this article,

describing a conversation between Lewis and Wreford, Lewis gives another account

of her parentage and childhood experiences, in which she both essentializes her

identities and sensationalizes her childhood experiences. She says, “My mother . . .

was a wild Indian.” She says that her father was a “negro” and a “gentleman’s

servant” who “saw and married” her mother.95 She says her mother is “of copper

color,” “with straight, black hair,” and in her 1873 interview with the Sun, she said she

got her eyes and hair from her mother.96 With her parents “buried from her sight,” she

was able to create their presence in a way that suited the identity she wanted to present

to the world. This narrative choice hid her parents from the audiences’ view, and in

this way, she could exercise some power over how much she chose to disclose about

them.97 Although the media projected their own ideas of indigeneity and blackness
