majority white audience from which she hoped to gain new patrons and buyers. My

reading supports and is supported by the arguments of John Ernest’s book, Chaotic

Justice: Rethinking African American Literary History and Kevin K. Gaines’ book,

Uplifting the Race Black Leadership, Politics, and Culture in the Twentieth Century

who cite writing techniques such as fictionalization and dissemblance as writing

tactics that Black nineteenth-century writers used to navigate the racial politics of their

audiences.

       Second, Edmonia Lewis’ interactions with African Americans and activists

matters. Chapter three is titled “Edmonia Lewis, Scholar Activist? The Education of a

Nineteenth-Century Artist” This chapter will engage these questions: Who was Lewis

as a reader? How did her education, formal and informal, impact her business acumen

and public identity? To what extent does her educational background and social

context shape her understanding of the world and her awareness of societal issues? To

answer these questions, I comment on Lewis’ education and social circles and connect

them to her business savvy as an adult. Lewis attended Oberlin University, where she

was exposed to literature.26 Later in life, she was connected to both white abolitionist

and Black activist circles. As a person who continued to read throughout her life, and

who did interviews for newspapers, she must have been aware of the international
attention she was receiving. In addition, several of her sculptures were her

interpretation of biblical and literary figures. This chapter suggests that there is more

room to discuss Lewis as a person versed in literature and politics.
