       Lewis exhibited five works in all. The Daily Evening Bulletin, a White-owned

San Francisco newspaper, listed the names and “lowest acceptable bids” of these

sculptures on September 6th, the Saturday after the exhibition began. The set included:

Hiawatha’s Marriage, for $550, a bust of Lincoln for $550, the complementary

sculptures, “Asleep” for $650 and “Awake” for $500, as well as Love in a Trap for

$500.279 The same article tells readers that quite a lot of people attended the

exhibition, and that Lewis herself would be present. Like the articles in the Pacific

Appeal, the Daily Evening Bulletin writers vouch for her character: “her pleasant face

and gentle and attractive manners are as interesting to many as the work of her

hands.”280 On September 6, the Pacific Appeal praised Lewis’ work as “magnificent,”

and as “none but a master of the statuary art can portray with the chisel.” This article

corroborates the Daily Evening Bulletin with more detail, noting that, “over one

hundred and fifty persons, white and colored, visited the Art Association rooms on

Monday the day of opening. The exhibition has been well attended during the

week.”281 Lewis sold Hiawatha and His Bride to a Mrs. C. L. Low for $550. Love
