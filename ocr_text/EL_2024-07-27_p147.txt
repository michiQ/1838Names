       Census data reveals that in the 1870s, there was a population of African

Americans who were able to attain wealth in the form of real estate and business

ownership. According to Douglas Henry Daniels, author of Pioneer Urbanites: A

Social and Cultural History of Black San Francisco, as of 1862, the African

Americans of San Francisco owned about $300,000 in assets, most of which was in

real estate.286 Edmonia Lewis visited San Francisco at a unique moment when the

youth of the city created economic opportunities for African Americans. The changes

brought about by the Gilded age siphoned these opportunities to the privileged classes,

bringing about a shift in the character and numbers of the Black population in San

Francisco. By 1873, San Francisco would have had over 1,000 Black residents.287

Even with this small population, they still formed several organizations and a

newspaper. During this period, they formed the California Colored Conventions, the

Executive Committee of San Francisco, the California Colored Citizens' State Union,

amongst others. Between 1867 and 1870, another Black-owned San Francisco

newspaper, the Elevator, published fifteen articles pertaining to Edmonia Lewis and

her progress. Prior to moving to San Francisco and publishing Pacific Appeal, editor
