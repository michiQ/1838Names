It has beea:wisely said by a Roman poet,
that “Life is not to live, but to be well.”
There are few, however, in. the possession of
good health, who duly appreciate its inesti-
mable blessings. Some from a neglect of
necessary exercise or from an over-exertion
of the mental, or physical powers, ruin the
fabric of their bodies. . Others from a want
of temperate habits, from excess of living, or
from indulgence of their licentious appetites,
lay themselves open toa train of diseases, the
consequences of which are insonsibly, per-
haps, though continually undermining their
system, and finally terminating in the decay
of their whole constitution.

The human frame is of such curious con-
texture, its formation so nice and regular, that
very small causes, ‘causes too which may ap-
pear altogether innocent, will often derange
all this admirable machinery,and ptoduce the
most alarming, and lasting effects. So few,
however, are consciaus, if conscious, regard-
Jess of this important certainty, that, they
pursue, and that incessantly, a course of life
which will most inevitably bring upon them
all the miseries of a shattered constitution,
and materially shorten the period of their
earthly existence. It is difficult for individu-
als, wheo the springs of motion are yet elas-
tic,—when the heart bounds with vigour, and
the eye sparkles with spirit, to conceive that
their sick day will ever overtake them. Such,
however, may learn the folly and_consequ-
ent wretchedness of profligate actions by
visiting the abode of the Valemidinarian,
whose system has been preyed, and gnawed
upon by a round of irregularities in life. Hear
