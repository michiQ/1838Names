In the midst of the contrary impulses of

alone evince the superiority; and strength of

manfully, and fealessly, the clearconclusions

Whether that strong, and ardentattachment
—attachment to the cause of truth, be exem-
plified in the character of the patriot, the phil-
losupher, or the philanthropist, it still presents
to the view, the appearance of that same un-
yielding, and magnanimous sentiment which
gathers around itself, and comprises within

itself evéry lofty, and noble exhibition of the}

mind, which can exalt, and, dignify the hu-
man character. In true moral courage there
isa dignity which involuntarily commands
the respect and admiration even of those who
possess it not. The coward is every where
contemptible, and to none more so,than him
before whose oppressive mandate he shrinks.
But if there be on earth, an object, who can
draw from all mankind’ an universal expres-
sion of admiration; an object upon which
angels would delight to gaze, and on which
even the majestic Author of our existence;
would deign to cast the smile of approbation
—it is that man, who firmly convinced that

the sentiments which he defends, are founded
in immutable truth, adheres to them with a

tenacity which nothing can relax—who, to
propagate these sentiments, stoops to no mean
and grovelling expedients, but stands forth in
all the native dignity, and independence of
inan, fearlessly bidding defiance to the ma-
levolent shafts of ignorance or prejudice—

world his sentiments, with his arguments to

i defend them.

As in every exhibition of the mental pow-
ers, the degree of its importance, or its insig-
nificance, ig measured in no small degree,
by the circumstances which attends its dis-
play, and the magnitude of the consequences
growing out of these circumstances, we are
to look for the most sublime spectacles of
moral courage, not in these men whose posts
in life are confined within narrow limits, but
in those who are placed in situations calcula-
ted to call forth the mightiest energies—.
where great interests are at stake, and the
destiny of individuals, or whole nations hang
upon the issue. We cannot point to a more
striking instance of true moral courage and
its effects, than we find in the character of the
illustrious Grecian Orator. That period of
his life in which he showed to the world
that he possessed that rarest endowment of
our species—true force of character, was when
in direct opposition to the wishes of the
Grecian populace he advised war against

Philip of Macedon. Demosthenes heard
