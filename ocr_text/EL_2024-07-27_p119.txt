                                         Chapter 4

      AROUND HALF THE WORLD IN THIRTY DAYS: EDMONIA LEWIS
                NAVIGATES THE BLACK ATLANTIC


                                     Introduction

        On a chilly fall evening in November of 1873, Edmonia Lewis sat in the parlor

of James Peck Thomas, a formerly enslaved person and one of the wealthiest African

Americans in St. Louis. Sitting with her by the fireside was a correspondent of the St.

Louis Globe. According to the correspondent, the home was lit by gas, and decorated

with elegant furniture. Lewis was in the city to bring a sculpture of the Virgin Mary to

a local church, and according to her interviewer, had “ . . . recently returned from

California, and will soon proceed to Rome.”229 In his closing line, he wrote “The lady

has crossed the Atlantic eight times, and does not seem appalled at the prospect of

having to sail over its stormy waters once more, even at the depth of winter.”230 In this

closing sentence, the correspondent underscores just how impressive Edmonia Lewis

was as a savvy and determined businesswoman and traveler and gives us just enough

information to open a line of inquiry about the intersections of race, gender, and travel

in the mid to late nineteenth century.
