that the school taught was the importance of physical labor to health and work ethic.

At the time Lewis was a student, a Mr. Luther Wellington oversaw the Manual Labor

Department. She could have interacted with him while completing her manual labor

requirement. Ms. Keziah King was the primary teacher for the school. Richardson,

writing about the instruction offered at McGrawville (New York Central College),

points out the following:

       In 1853, Miss King completed an educational experiment she had
       conducted for a year and a half to see, as she wrote, ‘whether colored
       boys and girls could learn as fast as white ones’ following her
       curriculum of elementary and remedial instruction. In her report to the
       trustees she answered the question in the affirmative.

While this experiment was completed three years before Lewis arrived, it provides

several pieces of contextual information. First, Kezia King had designed a curriculum

for groups deemed to be remedial learners, such as Lewis, who by her own account,

did not learn to read until she started at New York Central College. Second, the

trustees of the school were constantly faced with criticism and financial difficulties

and had to prove that an integrated school was a worthwhile enterprise, primarily

because nonwhite children were viewed as unintelligent. In a larger national context, it
was still illegal for those in bondage to learn to read or write at all. Noting these

things, it is less surprising that Lewis would play both with and against racist

ideologies in her future interviews.
