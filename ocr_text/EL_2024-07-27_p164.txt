       At this same convention, John Mercer Langston gave an address entitled “The

Relations of the Colored People to American Industry,” in which he alluded to Lewis:

       Here, too, it may be mentioned, with no inconsiderable pride, that one
       of the finest landscape painters of our country, and one of the finest
       sculptoresses is of African descent; the former distinguished especially
       as giving life and utterance from canvas to several of Milton's
       matchless poetical creations in the "Paradise Lost," and the other as
       making the spirit of the noble Andrew of Massachusetts to breathe and
       speak through the life-like lips and features of plaster.314

In his comments, Langston positioned Lewis as a documentarian who records shared

memory through her classic realism. To him, Bannister the landscape artist and Lewis

the sculptor were interlocutors in the realm of high art and literature, successfully

translating word to image. Their artistic ability disrupted the notion that African

Americans could not participate in so-called high arts and the humanities. For this

reason, these artists were celebrated as a credit to the race in many Black publications

and orations, both secular and religious. As Lewis’ career progressed, other Black

leaders spoke highly of her, advocating for her to be “recognized and encouraged” as

Langston suggested. Over the next several years, Lewis would continue to grow in her
