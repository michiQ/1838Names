               Miss Edmonia Lewis Returns: A Boston Homecoming

       Narrativizing Lewis’ experience as a traveler and a famous expatriate who

maintained connections to the United States helps to bring more attention to the

presence of African American women in the public sphere in the nineteenth century.

Elsa Barkley Brown disrupts the gendered public-private dichotomy in her 1994

article, “Negotiating and Transforming the Public Sphere: African American Political

Life in the Transition from Slavery to Freedom.” She was concerned that, “much of

the literature on the nineteenth-century public sphere constructs a masculine liberal

bourgeois public with a female counterpublic.”257 Since then, scholars have continued

to complicate our understanding of Black culture and the public sphere. Just over a

decade later, Martha S. Jones addressed, “the extent to which African American

women would exercise autonomy and authority within their community’s public

culture” in her book, All Bound Up Together: The Woman Question in African

American Public Culture, 1830-1900 (2007).258 Looking at Lewis’ travels and the

events she attended while in America also serves to disrupt the gendered public-

private dichotomy that these scholars call into question. When Lewis first returned to

America, she was still in her twenties, unmarried and formally educated, an
