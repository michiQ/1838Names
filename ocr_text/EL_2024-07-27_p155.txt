understanding Edmonia Lewis career creates a new chronology that adds value to her

experience in African America in a paradigm where her experience as an expatriate

would make that experience seem minor. We find this facet of Lewis’ life and career

with close analysis of nineteenth-century Black print. In this chapter, I will rely on

Black print to reconstruct the events around Lewis’ presentation of her magnum opus,

“Death of Cleopatra,” at the United States Centennial. This narrative brings attention

to the overlap between Lewis’ career and the efforts of African American leaders,

particularly the A.M.E church, to commemorate African American history during the

America’s first Centennial. In so doing, this chapter builds on the work of several

scholars who have studied African American presence at the Philadelphia Centennial

Exhibition, Lewis’ “Death of Cleopatra,” which she exhibited there, and African

American commemoration practices in the nineteenth-century. Philip Foner offers

important commentary on the presence of Edmonia Lewis and Frederick Douglass at

the Centennial in Philadelphia, and the discrimination African Americans faced at this

national event.299 Mary Frances Cordato’s essay outlines the efforts of (white) women

to be represented at the Philadelphia Centennial Exhibition, but does not give attention
