introduction to the subject matter familiar to other sculptors of her day. An 1878 New

York Times article entitled, “Seeking Equality Abroad” offers a narrative about this

time in Lewis’ life not found elsewhere. According to the article, Lewis visited Boston

prior to settling there. It was during this visit that the sculptures of the city caught her

attention, and inspired her to pursue art. She went to New York a few years later, and

“found a sympathizing listener in Rev. Highland Garnet, the friend and admirer of the

heroic John Brown.” The journalist wrote that “Next, armed with letters of

introduction, she called on William Lloyd Garrison, the veteran abolitionist.”200

Iterations of the same story were published in different newspapers. The New York

Times noted, “Some years later, she came to New York, and found friends in Rev.

Henry Highland Garnet and William Lloyd Garrison.”201 In Oberlin’s Annual Catalog,

Lewis is listed as being from New York City, and she herself said that she traveled to

New York City with her mother’s family during her childhood.202 Also important to

note: Reverend John Keep was in communication with William Lloyd Garrison at this

time.203 It is possible that the reverend set these introductions in motion.
