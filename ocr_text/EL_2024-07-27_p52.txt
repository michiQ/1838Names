Genesee Falls and Watkins Glen.”81 Lewis maintains that the seeds for her career

choice and her constant travel were sown in her childhood, while spending time with

her Native American family. By mentioning popular tourist destinations such as New

York City, Watkins Glen, Genesee Falls, and Niagara Falls, Lewis might have evoked

popular images of Native American makers and sellers in the minds of her readers.

       In her interview with Lydia Maria Child, Lewis drew attention to the

demarcation between Native and white American culture to exoticize herself. Child

asked Lewis if she liked life with her mother’s family. She answered, “Oh yes; I liked

it a great deal better than your civilized life.”82 She goes on to discuss her love of

nature as compared to life in the city:

       There is nothing so beautiful as the free forest. To catch a fish when
       you are hungry, cut the boughs of a tree, make a fire to roast it, and eat
       it in the open air, is the greatest of all luxuries. I would not stay pent up
       in cities if it were not for my passion for art.83

The ways in which Lewis describes her childhood reads as a confabulation of her

Native identity with popular conceptions of what it means to be a Native American.
