biographical sketch of Lewis read as a paraphrased compilation of the widely

circulated articles about her. The one line that Williams quotes directly, “I, too, can

make a stone man,” appears in several other published works about Edmonia

Lewis.151 The earliest use of this phrase appears in Laura Curtis Bullard’s April 1871

article. Shortly after, it was reprinted in Frederick Douglass’ New National Era and a

couple papers in Kansas.152 “I, too, can make a stone man,” seemed to be the catch

phrase used to describe Edmonia Lewis’ rise to fame. The phrase appears in Brown’s

1874 text, and Scrugg’s 1893 text as well as Henry F. Kletzing and William Henry

Crogman’s 1898 text, Progress of a Race, Or the Remarkable Advancement of the

American Negro.153 Twentieth century writers continued to use the phrase to describe

Lewis’ rise to fame, such as the 1907 text, Negro Neighbors, Bond and Free: Lessons
