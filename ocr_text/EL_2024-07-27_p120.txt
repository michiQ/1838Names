       That Lewis “did not seem appalled” by a winter journey to Rome speaks to her

fortitude and self-possession, but as a complex individual and a businesswoman with a

schedule for commissions, exhibitions, and other related responsibilities, there is also

room to assume that she also experienced a full range of emotions during the months

of travel across the United States and Europe. There are many ways that Lewis might

have sought solace from the difficult challenges she faced as a Black woman

entrepreneur. The smell of the salty air on a still morning at sea, dolphin sightings, or

the wide expanse of a clear starlit sky, the beautiful views of the plains and Mountains

of the Midwest, or even seeing the Pacific Ocean for the first time all might have been

moments of beauty, wonder, and joy that she experienced. The unexpected kindness of

a stranger and the warm welcome of friends also might have served as balms for her

when life was particularly difficult.

       Most of the writing on Lewis focuses on her relationship with the Brahmin and

abolitionist class in Boston, and the same upper class of white women and men with

whom she interacted in Rome. However, that story is falsely idyllic and incomplete.

While influential white leaders like William Lloyd Garrison, Wendell Phillips, and

Lydia Maria Child helped to bring Lewis into the spotlight, her experience in white

American circles was inconsistent. White women like Lydia Maria Child and Anne
Whitney have comprehensive archives of letters, while the archives of her Black

associates, like Garnet or Langston, are obtuse which creates this skewed story. These

letters provide some insight on the limitations of Lewis’ friendship with these women.

They gossiped about her in ways that betrayed their own racial prejudice.
