       Lewis’s academic education was coupled with a political education. When she

enrolled in 1856, New York Central College had 109 students, eighteen were black,

ten had been born slaves.184 She was enrolled for the second half of the 1856-1857

school year, as well as summer and fall of 1858. In 1852, just four years before Lewis

arrived, Charles Reason, the first African American to be hired as a professor at a

predominantly white college, left after three years of teaching Belles-letters, Greek,

Latin and French, as well as mathematics. Although Lewis’ time at Central College

did not overlap with Reason's, it is likely that she heard of him and the significance of

his presence. She would have close contact with formerly enslaved students, which

may have enlightened her to the plight of most African Americans in her community,

of which she may not have been familiar given her upbringing. Perhaps, the

experience of her Black peers, and the freedom work happening on her campus
