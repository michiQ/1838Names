The article written by Sampson was the only one the Christian Recorder published in

relation to her presence at the Centennial. While the interaction may have been

influenced by Sampson’s interpretive lens, if taken at face value, it points to Lewis’

experience of disappointment and frustration while exhibiting her work. Her

sentiments seemed to reflect those expressed by the convention committee that had

praised her years earlier. Her desire for encouragement from other African Americans

who “had been more favored,” sounds like what the Committee on Women’s Labor

wanted for Edmonia Lewis: “if cordially recognized and encouraged by colored men

in their more matured experience in these directions, would be the beginning of an era

. . .” Also, her comments very closely parallel the article Douglass wrote about her 3

years before. He noted that she “suffered many discouragements at the hands of her

own fellow countrymen.” After committing at least a year of physical, mental, and

emotional labor to her largest work, she said she received criticism from people “aping

the prejudices of the whites.”

       Lewis desired to show excellence in her profession and to represent her race,

which might be why she expressed frustration while conversing with J.P. Sampson.
