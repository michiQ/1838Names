  The Resolve of the Colored Women of America: African American Women’s
                      Ongoing Support of Edmonia Lewis

        The role of Black women in supporting Lewis’s career should not be

underestimated. As part of the centennial planning, there was to be another committee,

made up of women, that would raise the money for Edmonia to contribute a large-

scale work to the United States. I have not yet found any material recording their

efforts, but there is no doubt that women contributed to this effort, either by direct

fundraising efforts or by donating money themselves. Black women also did care work

for Lewis, not only when she arrived in Philadelphia, but throughout her career. In

July of 1876, the Christian Recorder published an article called “The Centennial: Our

Colored Visitors,” listing the African Americans who were boarding with Black

women in Philadelphia. As mentioned in the previous chapter, Mrs. C.E. Gilbert was

the successful proprietress of the Keystone Centennial House, In Philadelphia. In

1873, she advertised in the Christian Recorder that she acquired 625 Pine Street, the

house neighboring her 627 Pine location; the boarding house had been a familiar

location to travelers for some time. The boarding house was two doors down from the

office of the Recorder, making it a hub for A.M.E. Church members, affiliates, and

African Americans in general. Bishop Arnett, as well as a few reporters stayed with

her. Edmonia Lewis stayed with Mrs. Sarah Young who owned a boarding house on

1836 Lombard St. From conception to exhibition, the practice of commemoration was

a communal one, requiring cooperation across gender and place.
