    Preliminary Remarks: Edmonia Lewis in Black Women’s Historiography

       In her speech, “The Progress of Colored Women,” delivered in 1898 to the

National American Woman’s Suffrage Association meeting celebrating the
organization’s 15th anniversary in Washington D.C., then President Mary Church

Terell said the following:

       Ever since a book was published in 1773 entitled Poems on Various
       Subjects, Religious and Moral by Phillis Wheatley, Negro Servant of
       Mr. John Wheatley, of Boston, colored women have given abundant
       evidence of literary ability. In sculpture we were represented by a
       woman upon whose chisel Italy has set her seal of approval…1

The woman whose chisel Church Terell was referring to was internationally

recognized African American and Native American sculptor Edmonia Lewis. That

Church Terrell invokes Wheatley and Lewis to historicize the ongoing achievements

of Black women reveals that Lewis’ career was of great significance to late-nineteenth

century African Americans. Terrell’s invocation of Lewis also shows us that there was

an important intersection between African American women’s achievements in the

arts and humanities, and the goals of the late-nineteenth century Black women’s club
