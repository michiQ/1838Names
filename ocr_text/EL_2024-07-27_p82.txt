Yet, in employing one strategy to navigate the challenges imposed by racism and

sexism, Lewis was faced with another. In the same Frank Leslie article mentioned

previously, he starts with the sentence, “Edmonia Lewis is a little American girl,

scarcely 22 years of age…” And later he wrote: “Miss Lewis is by no means a

prodigy; she has great natural genius, originality, earnestness, and a simple, genuine

taste. Her works are yet those of a girl.”147 Her physical appearance would be

conflated with her identity, long after she was 22 years old.

       Her use of this story is one of several identity transformations, from “little

orphan sister,” to “Indian girl,” to “little friend,” and finally to something more

transcendent. The last line in How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist reads as follows:

“God's gift to Edmonia Lewis is unconquerable energy, as well as genius; and these

two combined enable her to rise above all prejudices of race or color, and command

the respect and honor of all true lovers of art.”148 Ending the narrative with a

statement that connects God and race evokes religious anti-slavery/pro-equality

discourse; in particular, the idea of all people being made in the likeness of God, or all

being God’s children.149 That Edmonia’s talents are gifts from God makes it possible
