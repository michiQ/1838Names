       Since Smith Foster’s Written by Herself, a significant collective of humanities

scholars have contributed to the study of nineteenth-century Black women’s writing,

and American literary history more broadly. Claudia Stokes maps out the social and

cultural landscape for the rise of literary history in her book, Writers in Retrospect:

The Rise of American Literary History, 1875–1910.40 Her contextualization of the

field, as well as the work of nineteenth-century bibliographers, helps me to feel

confident to add Edmonia Lewis to the story of American Literary History. I rely on

John Ernest’s framework for understanding the racial dynamics of nineteenth-century

literary history that he outlines in his book, Chaotic Justice: Rethinking African

American Literary History as a backdrop for my interpretive work. Ernest explores

the:

       Shifting instabilities of racial identity and cultural performance during
       the nineteenth century, and the ways in which African American
       literature produced throughout that century reveals various pressures
       that have directed the guiding currents of U.S. history.41
