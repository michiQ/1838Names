Americans were well represented.321 Unfortunately, none of the appropriations for the

Centennial would be used to acknowledge African Americans’ first century years

since U.S. independence. The African American community would have to raise their

own funds to commemorate their contributions to the United States.

       There were several Black institutions responsible for launching large-scale

fundraisers at this time: the Black Church, Black clubs and conventions, the Black

press. These entities, as well as individual Black activists and politicians, took

collective action to create their own commemorative event during the centennial. At

the time Douglass published his article about Lewis, there were a few African

American elected officials who were also editors: George Thompson Ruby, who was

part of the Texas Senate from 1870-1874 and editor Galveston Spectator, and P. B. S.

Pinchback, Lieutenant Governor of Louisiana and editor of the Louisianan.322 These

two men would use their influence as politicians and editors to call for further action.

A few months after Walls stood before Congress to argue in support of appropriating

funds to the Centennial Exhibition, P. B. S. Pinchback’s Louisianian made a call for a

convention of colored newspaper men.323 During this convention, there would be time

allotted to discuss Black participation in the Centennial.
