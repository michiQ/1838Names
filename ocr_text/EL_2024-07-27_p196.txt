While Lewis was not an educator, she contributed to reinforcing the validity of these

spaces by contributing to their aesthetic development. While she was in the United

States for the Centennial, she contacted African American institutions of higher

education to ask if they would be interested in having her busts of inspirational figures

such as John Brown, Charles Sumner, and Henry Wadsworth Longfellow. These busts

were made of plaster, then painted to resemble terra cotta, making them easy to afford

and create. They were $10, to cover shipping costs. For Centennial goers, they were

$8. The only correspondence that has been found thus far is a letter from Edmonia

Lewis to Samuel Chapman Armstrong at Hampton University.364 In addition, one of

her works is at Wilberforce. In October of 1876, the Memphis Daily Appeal made this

announcement: “Edmonia Lewis, the colored sculptor of this country, has presented

the Fisk university with terra cotta busts of John Brown, Charles Sumner, and H. W.

Longfellow.”365 Art was a representation of cultural attainment and advancement. By

contributing to the cultural development institutions for Black education, Lewis was

contributing to their viability and standing as institutions.
