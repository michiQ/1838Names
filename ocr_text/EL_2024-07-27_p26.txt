organized within a racist colonial society to ask and answer questions about the

humanity and affective experience of her Black women subjects, and in so doing,

deliberately displaces and transgresses its limits. I believe, to engage with Lewis

innovatively, I will need to transgress the apparent limits of her archive, to, as stated

by Brent Hayes Edwards, “‘both to tell an impossible story and to amplify the

impossibility of its telling.”24 My research has consisted of creating a unique

arrangement of—to borrow Edwards’ phrase—“orchestrated fragments” of archival

material related to Edmonia Lewis.25 Rather than resisting the silences and

fragmentation of the archive, I closely read the materials we have, which includes

periodicals, travel records, pamphlets, correspondence, books, and of course,

sculpture. Furthermore, I make use of academic records, entries in city directories, and

other material to create a narrative of her career and relation to literary and visual

communities. In this way, I consider the silences Lewis may have intentionally

created, while also leaving space for the ephemera that might have been

unintentionally lost or destroyed.
