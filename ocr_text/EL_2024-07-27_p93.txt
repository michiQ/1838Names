       In her 1866 interview with Henry Wreford Lewis declared, “I was then sent to

school for three years in McGraw, but was declared to be wild--they could do nothing

with me.”171 While this account may contain a certain amount of truth, also embedded

within is some amount of fictionalization. Yet, evidence exists that Lewis was not as

difficult and unruly as she lets on. It might have been Ms. King who graded Lewis’

performance. In her first term, she received all As, except for Grammar, in which she

received a 70. In another term in which she took grammar, she received an 85. By the

fall of 1858, she received a perfect score in every class besides arithmetic, in which

she received a 98.99. She also had consistently perfect scores in Declamation, which

is oration. The two most significant notes from her grade reports are: first, that the

summer term of 1858 she took a drawing class, in which she received a perfect score;

and second, that she received a perfect score for conduct in every semester but her

first, in which she received a 97.172 What’s more, the school was under the guidance

and support of some of the most respected activists and abolitionists, meaning that

Lewis was likely exposed to some of the most forward-thinking people of her day. In

the summer of 1858, Frederick Douglass visited the campus of New York Central

College to give a speech entitled, “Self Made Men” to its Literary Society. Douglass
