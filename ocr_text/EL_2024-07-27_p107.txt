founded the Liberator, pledging to be outspoken in his efforts to end slavery and

further the rights of Black people in America. Eventually, he gained readers across the

country. Some have attributed abolitionist papers like the Liberator with gradually

putting slavery at the top of the nation's agenda. By the end of 1831, he had fifteen

collaborators, and with that number, he felt confident in launching an anti-slavery

society. On January 6th of 1832, 12 men, all of whom were white, convened in the

schoolroom of the African Baptist Church on Beacon Hill, in which they completed

and signed the constitution for the New England Anti-Slavery Society. Zorn describes

some of its first initiatives:

        Four standing committees were directed to aid and protect Boston's
        colored citizenry, and the society initiated research reports on the slave
        trade, colonizationism, the status of American slavery, and the
        condition of the northern free Negro population. In addition, a
        memorial was sent to Congress requesting the abolition of slavery in
        the District of Columbia.205

As the organization grew, surrounding states formed their own anti-slavery societies,

and the New England Anti-Slavery Society became the Massachusetts Anti-Slavery

Society. While abolition was the central goal of the society, they also petitioned for

the end of social discrimination against African Americans. It was through the support
of Garrison directly, and his role as a founder of both the Liberator and the

Massachusetts Anti-Slavery Society, that Edmonia Lewis was able to get her start as

an artist. It was because of these institutions that Boston was the ideal place for Lewis

to build a network of patrons and clients.
