          Ernest’s reading of “crafted silences” and “strategic omissions” pairs well with

Gaines’ use of dissemblance, a term coined by Darlene Clark Hine in her essay, “Rape

and the Inner Lives of Black Women in the Middle West.” Hine originally defined

dissemblance as, “the behavior and attitudes of Black women that created the

appearance of openness and disclosure but actually shielded the truth of their inner

lives and selves from their oppressors.”52 In his discussion of dissemblance as a

“struggle for autonomous self-consciousness,” a “weapon,” and “psychic armor

enabling the survival of the powerless,” Gaines emphasizes dissemblance as a tactic,

rather than a defense.53 Given Lewis’ position as a free-born, formally educated

entrepreneur, I apply Gaines' use of dissemblance to my interpretation of How

Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist. The narrative contained in this pamphlet is

representative of a fictionalized (auto)biography that Lewis used throughout her career

to form an identity legible to mostly white readers, while also preserving her privacy.
