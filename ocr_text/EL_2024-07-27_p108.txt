        At around the same time, the first Colored Convention was hosted by Bishop

Richard Allen, the founder of the African Methodist Episcopal Church at Bethel AME

Church in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania in 1830. Garrison would be amongst the white

abolitionists to attend the First Annual Convention of the People of Colour in

Philadelphia in June of 1831. By the Second Annual Convention, Garrison would send

a letter on behalf of the New England Anti-Slavery Society, in which he congratulated

the convention, “on the probability, from present prospects, of [their] condition being

improved by the joint efforts of our friends and [them]selves.” The political program

of the New England Anti-Slavery Society included the use of literature, traveling

agents, and gatherings. Activists who were formerly enslaved, the most notable of

whom was Frederick Douglass, served as agents who would share their experiences of

slavery at public gatherings. Frederick Douglass would start several newspapers in

which he preached the message of anti-slavery and racial equality. He advertised and

participated in Colored Conventions, and there were Colored Convention minutes that

were printed in his offices in both Rochester, NY and later, Washington D.C.206 The

Colored Conventions, as a national network, and Frederick Douglass, both as an

individual and as a member of the Colored Conventions, also acted in support of

Lewis. Both the Colored Conventions, and the Massachusetts Anti-Slavery Society
would be well established political machines by the time Lewis left Oberlin College in

1863.
