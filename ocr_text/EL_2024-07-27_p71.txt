was part of Lewis’ experience as well. This certainly shaped Lewis’ understanding of

race relations and education. In an interview, Edmonia says that her first encounter

with education was the “black robes,” an Indian term for French Jesuit missionaries

popularized by Longfellow. According to Edmonia’s biographers, Henderson and

Henderson, she boarded with someone by the name of Captain S.R. Mills, and Samuel

paid for private tutors. However, the experience described in How Edmonia Lewis

Became an Artist seems to refer to either New York Central College, which she

attended from 1856-1858, or Oberlin College, which she attended from 1858-1863. It

reads as follows:

       Money was sent, clothing bought, and the Indian girl dressed up in a
       civilized dress. How many times she wished for the Indian costume, as
       she felt so strange in her new dress! How many times she fell down
       stairs, and sometimes up, too! But she must bid farewell to her
       Chippewa dress.124

A few lines later, the narrator states the following, “At school everything seemed

strange and new to her; but Edmonia soon made friends . . .” Edmonia’s assimilation

process would have seemed both comical and endearing to readers. Since most women

wore long, layered skirts at the time, it is possible that the experience of tripping on
stairways was relatable, even comical to some.
