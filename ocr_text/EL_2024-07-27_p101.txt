       Lewis left the Young Ladies’ Preparatory Department and enrolled in the

Young Ladies’ Course from 1860-1863. In Oberlin’s 1863 Annual Catalogue, she was

listed as a third-year student. She would have been studying Olmsted’s Natural

Philosophy, Mechanics and Hydrostatics, Anatomy and Physiology, Principles of

Zoology, and Systematic Zoology with Lectures, in her first term. For her second

term, she would take Natural Philosophy, Pneumatics, Acoustics, Electricity,

Magnetism and Optics, Chemistry, Whately’s Logic. In her third term she would have

taken Olmsted’s Astronomy, Logic completed, Whately’s Rhetoric, and Guizot’s

History of Civilization.192 The prescribed course schedule was just as varied for all

other academic levels. This robust liberal arts education points to how well-read Lewis

became during these years, not just in her familiarity with literature and history, but in

her exposure to multiple subjects. This exposure was fundamental to shaping Lewis

into someone who could interview well, travel alone, manage her own business, sculpt

marble without assistants, and manage, by most accounts, to be so self-possessed,

composed, and compelling.
