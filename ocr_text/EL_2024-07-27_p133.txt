       Edmonia Lewis’ career benefited from the rapid advancements in

transportation across the Western world, but it also suffered due to the extent to which

racism defined her experience as a traveler. She relied on people like James Peck

Thomas, and other African Americans of means, to provide comfortable lodging as

she traveled across the United States. Thomas’ mother, a formerly enslaved woman

named Sally Thomas, orchestrated her son’s freedom. Thomas gained his wealth as a

barber and through real estate, like Lewis’ brother, Samuel, He also had an

advantageous marriage to Antoinette Rutgers, the daughter of a wealthy Haitian

woman named Madame Pelagie Rutgers. He had spent the summer touring Europe,

where he learned of Edmonia Lewis, and contacted her. He commissioned Lewis to do

a bust of himself, as well as a memorial for the grave of Madame Rutgers, who died

before they were married.255 According to Lewis biographers, “From a clay model

made at their home, the Thomases ordered a marble statue of the Virgin Mary at the

cross with a base and pedestal.”256 This was the common course of Edmonia’s

experience when staying with friends and patrons. The Thomases also allowed Lewis

to take the interview described at the beginning of this chapter while in their St. Louis

home. The significance of a network of African Americans on whom she can rely for

lodging, as well as financial and emotional support, cannot be emphasized enough.
