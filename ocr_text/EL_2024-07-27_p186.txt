the question of why the convention delegates did not follow through on their initial

resolutions. Part of the resolution regarding Lewis was that “the committee be

instituted at the close of the Exposition, to place said work of art in some public

building, or park in the city of Washington, there to remain.” Unfortunately, this did

not happen. After her “Cleopatra” was exhibited in Chicago, it ended up on display in

a Chicago saloon.347 In addition, the multi-volume history the committee hoped to

have ready by the exhibition was not published. Edmonia Lewis’ “Death of Cleopatra”

was the only art by an African American in the Centennial. The only African

American exhibit was a statue called “The Freed Slave,” a work that was about

African Americans, but not created by an African American. Yet, after the centennial,

Peter H. Clark, Daniel A. Payne, and Benjamin W. Arnett personally supported Lewis

through their patronage. Clark purchased three busts—of John Brown, Charles

Sumner, and Henry Wadsworth Longfellow. Payne and Arnett both ordered busts of

themselves. In November of 1878, when Lewis was in Cincinnati sculpting a bust of

Payne, she stayed at Clark’s home, and attended his daughter’s wedding.348 So,

although the Black press initially involved with promoting Lewis were silent, the

individuals involved seemed to continue their relationships with her, an inconsistency

that is still more curious.
