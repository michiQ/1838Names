       Brown’s text was part of a larger effort among African Americans to use the

stories of “self-made” or “representative men” to disprove the generally accepted

notion that African Americans had no cultural attainments and were biologically

incapable of intellectual or artistic production. One of the most pressing political

imperatives for African American activists during the nineteenth century was to

contest these tenets of nineteenth-century racism, and promoting Black exceptionalism

was one way to do that. However, the focus on self-made men in nineteenth-century

discourse has had a lasting impact on Black historiography. Individual stories often

eclipse narratives that focus on the communal nature of history making. Frederick

Douglass’ famous “Self Made Man” speech, quoted above, brings attention to the fact

that a sense of collective progress existed alongside an effort to highlight individual

successes. This no doubt was the case for Edmonia Lewis. Even if Lewis did not hear

Douglass’ speech, or read Brown’s book, she was influenced by the zeitgeist of the

era, as she was one amongst this collective of African Americans who were following

their ambitions and using their talents to advocate for racial equality.297 It was Black
communities across the United States that worked collectively to provide financial,

social, and emotional support during her visits to the United States. It was Black
