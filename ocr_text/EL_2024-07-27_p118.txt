identity within a larger, complex, and contradictory, social context. During her youth,

she navigated the world without her parents, and most times, without her brother. She

lived on two college campuses as one of few nonwhite students at a time when

formally educating nonwhite students was part of an effort ‘civilize the subhuman

populations,’ as was the case for Native Americans, or a questionable experiment, as

was the case for African Americans. As part of the early generation of formally

educated nonwhite women, Lewis was living proof of the false logic of racism. The

progressive enclave in which she found support and opportunity, such as the white

abolitionists of Boston, were still characterized by specific mores and forms of

expression, many of which reveal deeply entrenched racist and sexist beliefs. These

experiences probably gave her the incite and motivation to leave America and create

her future elsewhere. However, the growth of Lewis’ network in America, and her

ability to navigate the social spaces that would lead her to lucrative opportunities,

were entirely dependent on her ability to adapt and communicate.
