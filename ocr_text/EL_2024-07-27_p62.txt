mainstream definitions of art in order to justify her career choice, and to critique the

assumed hierarchies that define civilized against uncivilized. Lewis showed

awareness of her audience and intentionality with how she shared her personal

narrative since the beginning of her career. In positioning herself as more influenced

by her Native American upbringing than her African American identity, Lewis crafted

a narrative in which she shifts from the position of uncivilized “Indian” to civilized

American. If she were aware of her audience, most of whom were white, she would

have known that positioning herself as a Native person who was able to be civilized

would serve the assumptions of her audience, and thereby gain their attention. While it

may be true that Lewis’ art practice was influenced by her childhood in a family of

craftspeople, by attributing her creativity to her mother, she projects the image of

picturesque, exotic craft makers onto herself, yet differentiates herself from it by

calling her art making “more civilized.”103
