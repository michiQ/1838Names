By adopting the narrative arch of the Pocahontas narrative, Lewis solved the linguistic

conundrum of her perceived place in society. In this case, she incorporated strategic

silences, as well as familiar tropes and narrative arcs to, as Ernest says, to tell “stories

beyond what white audiences were prepared to hear or capable of understanding.”


           “Our Little Friend:” Making a Sentimental (Nonwhite) Orphan

         There are terms of endearment in How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist that

serve to elicit sympathy from the reader. For example, the phrase “little friend,” used

in the sentence, “Little they thought that their little friend, who was running about at

play with them, would become an artist.” Together, the descriptions, “little orphan

sister,” “orphan girl,” and “little friend” pare Lewis down in size and age. With

additional gender descriptors, these phrases emphasize Edmonia’s vulnerability and

elicit popular imagery related to these phrases. The visual culture of the period

popularized the archetype of the vulnerable orphan girl. Sentimental images, designed

to emphasize the youth, innocence, and the woe of orphan characters appeared on the

frontispiece of such novels, in illustrated newspapers, and in art. Before Lewis’ time,

images such as the frontispiece for the 1827 story “Motherless Mary,” or the 1834

engraving by J. Romney, with the caption “Two homeless orphans (a girl and a young

woman) singing ballads to a family,” sought to elicit the viewer’s pity, sympathy, and
