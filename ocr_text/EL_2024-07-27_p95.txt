morally lacking would persist for many decades. Perhaps, in hiding the truth of her

intellectual and social capabilities, Lewis thought it would be possible to attract more

interest, and less resistance from her mostly white audience. Even after she gained

international recognition, she would still be under scrutiny. When Lewis visited

Chicago in 1870 to exhibit “Hagar in the Wilderness,” she was assaulted by a man

who did not believe that Hagar was the work of an African and Native American

person.177 It seems that Lewis might have known that most people wouldn’t believe

her work was her own. Unlike most of her white contemporaries, she did not hire

assistants to help her in her studio until later in her career, meaning she, a woman

often noted for her small stature, had to do all the manual labor involved with carving

marble herself.178 While it is possible that she could not hire assistants due to financial

constraints, it is just as likely that she chose to take on all the labor involved in

creating marble sculptures because she wanted to assert her authenticity as much as

possible. That she was an ignorant girl who stumbled into sculpture would have been

more digestible than the truth that she was an intelligent woman who intentionally

shaped her career.
