Lewis had to employ (auto)biography to appeal to her audience and thereby establish

her career, as she would not be easily accepted into high society without an

explanation of her background, especially given that she was the only non-white

person practicing in her field.54 To that end, my reading may explain how Lewis

became affiliated with Harvard University Alumni and why her ephemera and art has

been preserved by Harvard University, one of America’s most elite and selective

institutions. In her monograph on Lewis, Kirsten Pai Buick actively shifts her focus

away from Lewis’ biography. Her explanation is as follows:

       While the craft of biography attempts a verisimilitude of the lived
       experience, a kind of fictional transparency that can be laid over a life,
       the writing of the career adheres to different rules. The career stands as
       the medium between the artist as an individual and the artist as a
       product of culture.55

This is partially true; I too have focused on Lewis’ career in this project because it

allows me to look at Lewis’ cultural production and the social practices and networks

that allowed her career to flourish. However, the existence and the potentially wide

circulation of How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist, a biographical work, disrupts

this neat divide. Lewis’ career was intertwined with her identity and individual

experience. As Ernest noted, the “chaotic constructions of race shape individual lives,”
and in Lewis’s case, her individual life was a product of her cultural moment.56 How
