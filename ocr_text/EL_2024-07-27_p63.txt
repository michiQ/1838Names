seemed to be part of a larger effort to distance herself from the negative stereotypes

attached to African Americans. Lewis’ references to her father and her African

American identity are poignant to understanding her public persona. Given popular

interpretations of what it means to be Native American as compared to what it means

to be African American, there are contexts in which it would have been easier for

Lewis to market herself as more Native than African. And yet, Lewis seemed to want

to distance herself from all negative stereotypes associated with people of color to

position herself as “civilized.”

         “One of my dearest friends in London is Amelia B. Edwards, the well-
         known novelist, an amiable, kind creature. She once said to me, ‘You
         dear Edmonia, I think it is such a beautiful thing for you to come right
         away from those Indians, and make such a stand as you have done.’”106

Lewis made other commentary that might be shocking or confusing to the modern

reader. There is a part of the previously mentioned quote from the 1873 interview with

the San Francisco Chronicle that is relevant here, “. . .I almost envied the freedom of

the Indians which I saw on the plains! But then they were so dirty. I didn't like that in

them.”
