       In a report of the Cincinnati Enquirer in 1879, she was quoted as saying,

       “‘My father was not only a colored man,’ said Miss Lewis to our
       reporter, ‘but he was a black man, black as your hat, and a pure-
       blooded negro. My mother had nothing but Indian blood in her
       veins.’”98

This comparative statement she made about her parents could have been a reiteration

of hearsay or a decision to distance herself from the negative stereotypes associated

with Blackness, while aligning herself with the socially accepted stereotypes

associated with being a Native person. In an 1878 Indianapolis News interview, Lewis

was quoted thus, “‘My features,’ said she, ‘I take from my father, but my spirit, my

industry, and perseverance I get from my Indian mother.’”99 The traits Lewis uses to

describe her mother draw on the trope of the noble savage. Through existing logics of

miscegenation and inheritance, if Lewis identified with her mother, she could also be

identified with this mythical, socially acceptable conception of the Native American.
