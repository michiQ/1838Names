     EDMONIA LEWIS’ ENGAGEMENT WITH HER PUBLIC: A CLOSE
       READING OF HOW EDMONIA LEWIS BECAME AN ARTIST


       Slavery, Abolition, Emancipation, and Freedom: Primary Sources from

Houghton Library is an open access digital collection of “materials related to Black

history and culture” on Harvard’s Houghton Library website. The collection, divided
thematically and by period, has a section titled “Women’s Voices.” In this section,

there are four texts listed as “Poetry and Creative Works,” including Frances Ellen

Watkins Harper’s Poems on Miscellaneous Subjects (1854), Harriet Wilson’s, Our

Nig, or, Sketches from the Life of a Free Black (1859), and Emma, Dunham Kelley’s,

Megda, (1891). There is also a text, without an author listed, titled How Edmonia

Lewis Became an Artist. Charles Sumner donated the copy of How Edmonia Lewis

Became an Artist available at Harvard University’s Houghton Library on November 1,

1870.29 What does it mean for this text, that summarizes the early life of an native and

African American artist, Edmonia Lewis, to be listed alongside texts by African

American women writers? Indeed, what does it mean for our understanding of African

American and Indigenous literature to consider How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist

to be a literary work?
