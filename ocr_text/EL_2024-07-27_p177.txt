services or colored conventions, the reading of the newspaper itself was a communal

practice. Church announcements, and other forms of oral communication were

common practice. This gives added significance Lewis’ visits to African American

churches. For this very reason, the first order of business was the reading of

correspondence from respected leaders. Some of the letters that were read were from

Lewis’ contacts: Frederick Douglass, Peter Anderson, and Rev. Daniel A. Payne.

       The Committee on Centennial Celebration consisted of Bishop Benjamin W.

Arnett, P.B.S. Pinchback, and Rev. Henry McNeal Turner. Bishop Benjamin William

Arnett became the pastor of Allen Temple two years before the convention, in 1873,

and had been collaborating with Clark, Douglass, and others who supported Lewis

throughout her career.330 In the committee’s report, they outlined the “departments”

of a multi-volume history they planned to publish. Much like the history William

Wells Brown had published in 1874, just a year before, this centennial history was to

be comprehensive, and was to cover African American achievement in every domain.
