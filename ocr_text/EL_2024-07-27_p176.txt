         . . . Though the call for the convention emanated from editors of

It was not uncommon for black professionals to take numerous roles throughout their

lives, because of both urgency and personal interest. But what is even more significant

here is the role clergy played in empowering both the reading and non-reading publics

in their sphere of influence. In other words:

        You cannot get the colored men together by newspaper notices, many
        of them are unable to read newspapers, people who have just come
        from slavery know nothing about the notices: the proper way is to have
        the notices read in the churches and be circulated by the leading
        colored men.329

        The African American church served as not only a religious, but also a

political and educational institution. The church was vital to the dissemination and

proliferation of literature, and religious leaders were often the literate, trusted, and

respected people in African American communities. Literacy correlated to political
power, and the limited literacy of the Black community at this time shaped Black
