middle-class consciousness was multifaceted and contingent, representing a myriad of

responses under changing historical circumstances to the fundamental ambivalence of

being black in America.”42 The dissonance between the facts of Lewis’ experience and

the narrative points in her pamphlet point to the chaos, instability, and ambivalence

that Gaines and Ernest refer to in their texts.

          Lewis was exposed to literature while studying at New York Central College

and Oberlin College and the implications of this education for her career. As Marylin

Richardson explains in her article, “Edmonia Lewis at McGrawville: The Early

Education of a Nineteenth-Century Black Woman Artist,” Edmonia Lewis was well

read and received a liberal arts education, and archival documents show that she

maintained a strong academic performance throughout her education. However, she

minimized these aspects of herself in interviews and recorded conversations with

visitors to her studio. Lewis scholars Kirsten Pai Buick, Harry Henderson and Albert

Henderson, and Marilyn Richardson and Naurice Frank Woods have contributed to

recovering Lewis’ biographical chronology, while also locating the political and social

contexts that would prompt Lewis' to offer autobiographical narrative that is notably

dissonant with her personal history. As a literary scholar, How Edmonia Lewis

Became an Artist offers me and opportunity to use close reading and intertextual
analysis to explore this dissonance further. Giving greater significance to this

pamphlet can reframe how we think about Lewis’ public persona because it allows me

to hypothesize Edmonia Lewis as the author of her identity. It also allows us to think

more about her engagement with print culture and the history of Black print practice.
