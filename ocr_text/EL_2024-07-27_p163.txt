                  John Mercer Langston and the Colored Conventions

       Edmonia Lewis’ work ethic and ambition her an example of the vision of racial

uplift of Black women created by nineteenth century Black leaders. As Frances Smith

Foster wrote in her 1993 book, Written by Herself: Literary Production by African

American Women, 1746-1892, “The barriers against women's public presence and

particularly against their leadership in political and social issues were more easily
scaled when supported by others.”312 John Mercer Langston was one of these

supporters. While his name was not mentioned in descriptions of her 1869 visit to the

United States, given his long history with Lewis, it was almost certain that he was

made aware of her comings and goings. Langston, and other participants in the

Colored Conventions, recognized the significance of her progress as the only woman

artist of color practicing a high art form. In the “Proceedings of the Colored National

Labor convention: held in Washington, D.C., on December 6th, 7th, 8th, 9th and 10th,

1869,” a committee designated to report about women’s labor listed several

individuals who had ventured into “hitherto forbidden paths of duty or interest with

distinguished success:”

       Miss Edmonia Lewis among sculptors, Mrs. S.M. Douglass and Miss Cole

among colored women which, if cordially recognized and encouraged by colored men
