       Caroll calls the absence of parents “a neat narrative trick” that allows a “child

character unprecedented levels of freedom, increased agency and the opportunity to

have meaningful adventures free from adult oversight.”142 This may be true for

orphaned children who are white, but for the non-white orphaned child, there are

stipulations. Edmonia’s “happy,” “wild,” and “roving” life is racialized. Her

relationship to time and space is viewed as uncivilized, not a form of freedom and

agency. She is moving through space in ways commonly attributed to being an Indian

girl, not necessarily due to her status as an orphan. Every decision she makes

thereafter exists within the framework of assimilation into a Eurocentric American

society, one where she “bid farewell to her Chippewa dress.”

       In How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist, a young, non-white main character

who feigns ignorance plays on the possible paternalism of white readers who may

empathize with the plight of Native and Black people, but who played into the racial

hierarchy of the day. Sociologists have developed a formal definition for “playing

dumb” which applies to the self-fashioning strategies that were employed to write this

narrative. In sociology, playing dumb entails “pretending not to understand the other,
