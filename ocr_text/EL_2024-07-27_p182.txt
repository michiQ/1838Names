During the period that the Christian Recorder solicited funds for the Richard Allen

monument, there was only one direct mention of Edmonia Lewis. Perhaps this gap in

documentation exists because Lewis did in fact raise the money for own sculpture.

There are many questions to be answered here. What is clear is that the AME church

seems to have a great deal of trouble soliciting the funds they need for the

commemoration work they set out to do during the Colored Conventions.

       Even after the exhibition began, the Christian Recorder had little to say about

Lewis’ largest and most well-known work. Yet, there were several African Americans

who went to the Centennial, and one of them was John P. Sampson, a lawyer who

founded the Colored Citizen, a Cincinnati Newspaper. His brother, Benjamin Kellogg

Sampson, was a senior at Oberlin the year Lewis started the ladies’ course there. Both

brothers would teach at Wilberforce, and J.P. Sampson would serve on Wilberforce’s

Board of Trustees with Payne and others.343 After J.P. Sampson visited the centennial,

he wrote an article for the Christian Recorder called “Doing the Centennial.” In it, he
