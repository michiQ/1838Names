Holland makes an argument that may explain the contradictions and complexities of

Lewis’ public statements. Holland cites Rayna Green’s study of Native American

women, "The Pocahontas Perplex,” explaining it as follows:

         She [Lewis] could either be a Pocahontas-like figure, the cherished
         noble princess who renounces her heritage to save the white man and
         embraces his culture, or the negative image of the squaw, often
         portrayed as fat, indolent, alcoholic, and a prolific breeder, who
         satisfies the lusts of white men.107

Much like the mammy, pickaninny (such as Topsy), and minstrel, these archetypes

employed “codified images that reaffirmed the superiority of the dominant culture,”

and was thereby more tolerable to white audiences.108 It may not be so much that

Lewis was contradictory, but that she was trying to communicate in a system of

language and being that was illogical. She chose to work with and against larger

conceptions of both her identities to appeal to a largely white audience. However,

contextual and archival evidence shows that Lewis was equally aware of her position

as an African American woman, and her embodied experiences as an African

American making it difficult for her to be completely disconnected from this part of

her identity.
