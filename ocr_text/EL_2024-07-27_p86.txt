forms of print media, and job printing as a significant source for understanding Black

literary history and practice. Edmonia Lewis’ pamphlet allows us to trace the widely

circulated biographical details regarding Lewis to an originating source. If Edmonia

Lewis was indeed the author of this booklet, then it is essential to understand its

purpose and contents. Even if Lewis is not the author, she allowed its contents to

circulate as part of her own interviews, in newspaper articles, and other print materials

for several decades, which indicates that this pamphlet represented the public persona

she wanted to be associated with. Assumably, this text was in circulation along with

her visiting cards, giving us a new avenue to explore her process of self-fashioning

and self-articulation. While Lewis herself was not a printer, How Edmonia Lewis

Became an Artist is a pamphlet that does not list an author, date, or publisher. Likely,

Lewis contracted a job printer to produce and reproduce this piece of media through

the 1870s, classifying her as a “reader, maker, and user of different kinds of print,” in

the words of McHenry. To that end, this chapter is engaged with the work of

McHenry, whose projects unsettle and expand the scope of Black literature through

the study of a variety of print materials that are not normally considered to be

literature.
