         The market equivalence created between cotton and Black bodies
         galvanized a hermeneutics of the surface around Blackness that gave
         rise to a condition of viewership whereby Black bodies could (and had
         to) be read as embodiments of the commodity form.76

While Arabindan-Kesson’s study of visual representations of cotton may not directly

apply to Lewis, a free Black and Native woman who grew up in the north, her larger

contribution to the study of race and representation is useful here. Lewis’ approach to

selling her work shows that she understood how her body was read and manipulated

this hermeneutic of race and capital. To make plain the visual economy in which

Lewis was engaged, I would like to excerpt a description from the San Francisco

Chronicle describing the Santa Clara Valley Fair and Races of 1873. The reporter

wrote earlier in the article:

         Passing down the aisle on one hand are booths on which are the most
         beautiful collections of natural flowers and plants tastefully arranged
         rustic work, home-preserved sealed case goods, fruits and pickles,
         brilliant displays of the milliner's art silk and woolens of domestic
         manufacture, fruits and paintings in crayon and Watercolors and a fine
         collections of Oriental curiosities the whole terminating at the foot of
         the aisle with the wigwam occupied by Miss Edmonia Lewis
         containing some of her finest pieces of her handiwork from her studio
         in Rome.77
