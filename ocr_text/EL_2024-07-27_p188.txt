Turner had a lot of influence, and if there was a common sentiment that Lewis’

sculpture was not a suitable representation of the physical appearance of “colored

women of America,” this might have been the reason why there was no initiative to

find a place for it in Washington D.C. Perhaps Turner and other leaders of the time

sought representation that would represent Black women with African features.

Turner’s interpretation of Lewis’ presence at both the Centennial Exhibition and the

World’s Columbian Exposition can be read as a denial of her connection to America.

Between 1876 and 1895, African Americans saw the end of Reconstruction, the rise of

the Ku Klux Klan, and an ongoing increase in extrajudicial and state violence. Lewis’

visits to the United States lessened, while the stakes increased for African American

activists and leaders. Racial oppression and suppression-imposed limitations that

prevented her, Henry Ossawa Tanner, and other early African Americans (i.e. Anna J.
Cooper and W.E.B Dubois sought additional professional development in Europe)

from practicing in America served as an impetus for African Americans to act

collectively. In the almost twenty years since the Centennial, there was almost no

progress toward receiving representation and recognition during national events. So,

frustration was fomenting into anger.
