nineteenth-century Black women writers attended to their readership. In chapter six of

her book, titled “Writing Across the Color Line,” Smith Foster asserts that Black

women writers had to “negotiate” “labyrinthine intricacies” as part of her analysis of

readership and Harriet Jacob’s autobiography, Linda, or Incidents in the Life of a

       Consensus over who speaks of what to whom on what basis and in
       what manner are not requirements unique to African American women
       writers nor, as previous chapters illustrate, to the mid nineteenth
       century. But for African American women writing just before the Civil
       War, the particular configurations of racism and sexism made such
       consensus more difficult to gain.39

As will be evidenced by my analysis of How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist, this

would continue to be true after the Civil War as well. She was intentional in

fashioning her identity to fit her intended audience in order to market herself and her

work, while also preserving her authentic self and real experiences for her private life.

The broader questions animating my exploration in this chapter are as follows: Given

the format and length of this narrative, what plot points did Lewis include, omit, and

to what end? What does her narrative tell us about her intended audience, and her

knowledge of the genres of her contemporary moment? What was the potential

commercial value of the persona that this narrative helped her to solidify, and what

does it tell us about literary history in the late nineteenth century? Finally, how does

How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist contribute to our understanding of nineteenth-

century Black women’s literature?
