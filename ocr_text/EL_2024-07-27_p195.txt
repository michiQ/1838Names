Williams was also a politician and historian. In 1882, he published The History of the

Negro Race in America 1619–1880. This two-volume history was comprehensive,

over 1,000 pages in total. Part 8 of volume II, “The First Decade of Freedom,”

contains a chapter entitled “Representative Colored Men” (which does include

women). As mentioned earlier, Brown’s last chapter of The Rising Son was entitled,

“Representative Men and Women.” One of the notable differences between the two

works, however, has to do with the change brought about by twenty years. Brown’s

second to last chapter was entitled “The New Era,” and the last part of William’s

history is called “The Decline of Negro Governments.” In the last part of William’s

history, he focuses on Southern politics and Black migration to the North.362

        Brown used the phrase, “The New Era” to periodize the reconstruction years.

This chapter title is nearly the same as the name of Frederick Douglass’ paper the New

National Era, which was in publication from 1870-1874. What has been termed, post-

Emancipation, post-bellum, or Reconstruction, was, in African American letters, the

New Era. In his chapter entitled “New Era”, William Wells Brown made the following

call:

        Education is what we now need, and education we must have, at all
        hazards. Wilberforce and Avery Colleges, and Lincoln University, have
        all done good service. Howard University, Lincoln Institute, Hampton
        Manual Labor School, and Fisk University, are harbingers of light to
        our people. But we need an educated ministry; and until we have it, the
        masses will grope in darkness.363
