         It was the efforts of Black women that brought Lewis’ works to the 1893

World’s Fair. The Black women on the planning committee for Allegheny County

wanted to commission Lewis to create a representative bust for the World’s

Exposition and sent a letter to her dated January 10th, 1893. Lewis sent a response to

Ms. McCandless, a member of this committee, which was published in the Detroit

Plaindealer. The tone of her response tells us more about her sentiments: “In the first

place, allow me to thank you for your efforts on behalf of the colored women of your

city. They should be represented at the World's Fair . . .” She also said, “...after many

years of my life are passed and never before have the colored people given me such

recognition.”353 Apparently, Edmonia felt slighted, because she did not count “Death

of Cleopatra” as a formal recognition by African Americans. The women asked Lewis

to do a bust of Phillis Wheatly, but unfortunately, through some miscommunication,

the bust did not arrive in time.354 However, there were a few works by Lewis at the

Exposition. As mentioned in chapter two, Imogene Howard, who also was involved in

the Pennsylvania representation at the fair, loaned a copy of Edmonia’s Hiawatha to

display in the library of the Woman’s Building.355 Black women, as a collective, were

consistent in supporting Edmonia.
