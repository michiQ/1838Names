From this imperfect and brief sketch of the
public character of William Wilberforce,

that he was misled by the benevolence of his
nature, All appreciated his motives ; all aJl-
mired his talents, and revered his patriotism.
With whom shall we compare him? shall
we seek a parallel in’ the warrior, the philos-
opher, or the patriot of antiquity—for surely
none of modern times, however eminent, can
conepete with him for excellence. Shall we
call from anon the ancient dead, the Fabii,
and Cato, and Pompey, and Alexander, and
Ceasar? In these we frequently see traits
of courage and fortitude, which emblazon
their names on the page of history. But
their path to fame was stained with blood ;
ambition’s lustful fire moved them to deeds,
the renown of which dazzled their cotempo-
raries. Aud whatever degree of honor, or
glory, or profit, may lave accrued to their
yespective nations, it was purchased with hu-
man blood and human life. But our hero
was invulnerable to ambition ; and yet in his
career. ite shone a cloudless sun.

With respect to his philosophy—it was of
a noble cast. It was, that all men are by
nature equal; that they are wisely and justly
endowed by the Creator with certain rights,
which are irrefragable ; and that however hu-
man pride and human avarice may depress
and dehase, still, God is tne author of good
to man—and of evil, man is the artificer to
himself and to Lis species. Unlike Plato
and Sverates, his mind was free from the
gloom that surrounded theirs.

His philosophy was founded in the school
of christianity : for be it known that Mr. Wil-
berforee was a christian.
an exemplary and devoted member of the es-
tablished church; but at the time when reli-
gion was only noticed in the higher circles to
be scoffed at, he became the author of a work
entitlad “A Popular View of Christianity.”
A work pronounced to be ‘one of the most
valuable and useful publications of this or
any age, and which has been rendered the
instrument of religious benefit to multitudes
of per-ons, who could not be induced to look
into any religious book which came before
them less strongly recommended.” By his
writings and example in favor of moral re-
form, it is said that “he established around
hun a cirele of pious men, which has gradu-
ally, but constantly been extending itself, till
it has at length included within it many dis-
tinguished characters in every class of life,
political, literary, and seicntilic.” What a
weight of character this individual must have
pos-essed, if under such circumstances, he
could have influenced polite society to reli-
gious reading and meditation, And how
uniformly consistent he must have been to
have taken the stand of ‘ Leader of the reli-
gious world."—-IIow very unlike many sor-
did professors of the day, and their time-ser-

some future age, this stain on the page of

blush at the discrepancy between American
profession and American principle.
