in 1855, missionaries Asher and Laura Wright established the Thomas Asylum for

Orphaned and Destitute Indian Children, located on the Cattaraugus Indian

Reservation in New York State.121 Her brother Samuel, who was at a “school for

Indian boys,” could have been one of many children affected by this process of forced

assimilation. In 1867, a “Joint special committee to inquire into the condition of the

Indian Tribes” published a report with the following inquiry:

       What proportion of the children are orphans, and to what extent would
       it be practicable for the Indian Bureau to place orphan children in the
       families of Christian white men, to be trained and educated in the
       English language and in the habits of civilized life?122

Framed as a leading question, this inquiry and the answers described in the report

gives us a sense of the government’s perspective on native children without parents.

American views on assimilation culminated in Captain Richard Henry Pratt's 1892

speech at National Conference of Charities and Correction, in which he stated, "Kill

the Indian in him, and save the man." Although postdating the narrative in Edmonia’s

miniature book, her words--“But she must bid farewell to her Chippewa dress” --has a

similar connotation.
