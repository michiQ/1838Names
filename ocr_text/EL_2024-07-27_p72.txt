       When describing her transition from life with her tribe to life as a student, in an

American school in an 1873 interview with the Daily Graphic she said, “I felt the

strangest sensations in putting on dresses. I had never worn anything but blankets.

(laughing). You see I had good opportunities for studying the nude.”125 Lewis played

into the Victorian sentiments against the impropriety of Native people to invoke

humor, while at the same time attributing a skill key to her career as a producer of so-

called high art to her experience as a Native person. In another interview later that

year, Lewis invokes humor once again. The journalist describes it thus:

       She made some mirthful allusions to the squaws that she saw on the
       plains in coming from California, and said she would have been
       pleased to go with them if she could have followed her profession. She
       was afraid, however, that they would have broken her statuary as soon
       as they have a war dance.126

Her use of humor would work to disarm her audience, of whom a portion had

reservations and prejudices against a nonwhite, woman artist practicing in the

European tradition. Turning such racist stereotypes into the source of amusement

could have also worked to deflect insult, an act of poise that helped to turn attention

away from her racial identity and towards her personality.
