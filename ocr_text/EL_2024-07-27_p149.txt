dramatic flourish, however, there is truth that can be gleaned from this retelling. The

interviewer wrote:

       As I was passing the International Hotel yesterday morning, my eyes
       were arrested by a throng of men, women, and children, who stood
       around the doorway of the ladies’ entrance . . . I turned to a lady
       standing near and asked the meaning of the assemblage. “Why, don’t
       you know?” she cried. “That nigger woman has just come in.”290

After the interviewer found it was Edmonia Lewis who had attracted such a crowd, he

requested a meeting with her. The correspondent wrote that the ladies and gentlemen

in the “shabby-genteel” hotel parlor, “sat and stared at us as though we were subjects

of legitimate study. It was evident that Miss Lewis was a lion in the house.

Occasionally a party would appear at the door, gaze at her for a moment, and then pass

along.” The correspondent was both self-conscious and not fully cognizant of what

they were experiencing. He wrote, “Altogether this interview in public was a difficult

journalistic feat, and but for the natural ease and lady-like bearing of the sculptress

might have proved somewhat embarrassing.” A few lines later he wrote, “She sat

gracefully, answering all questions with a pleasant smile . . .”291 It was Lewis’ poise

that preserved the nerves of her interviewer, who was, assumably, a white man. It does

not seem that the interviewer had any understanding of the suppression and
humiliation Black women travelers faced due to this statement: “The International is a

fourth-rate hotel in Kearney Street, directly opposite the notorious Bella Union

Theatre, and just on the boundary of Barbary Coast, the Five Points of San Francisco.
