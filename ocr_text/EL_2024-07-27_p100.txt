Of the 3,000 residents 20% were African American, and of the 800 students, 5%, or 40

students were African American.189 In the words of Gary Kornblith and Carol Lasser,

authors of Elusive Utopia: The Struggle for Racial Equality in Oberlin, Ohio, Oberlin,

the town and the campus became, “an experiment dedicated to radical racial

egalitarianism,” inspired regional interpretations of Christian idealism.190 This ethic of

Christian egalitarianism attracted fugitives and free persons of color hoping to start a

new life, as well as generations of academically inclined African Americans who

would later be listed as firsts and greats. Edmonia Lewis was a part of the small

collective of African American Oberlin graduates who would be able to take

advantage of the opportunities created by Reconstruction, many of which would

disappear after the end of Reconstruction in 1877. In 1900, approximately one-third of

African American professionals had a degree from Oberlin.191 Black feminist thinkers

and Oberlin graduates Mary Church Terrell and Anna Julia Cooper, would write about

Lewis. Mary Jane Patterson, the first African American woman to attain a Bachelor of

Arts from an American college, graduated in 1862, a year before Lewis left Oberlin.
