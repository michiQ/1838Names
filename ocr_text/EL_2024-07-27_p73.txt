for their assumed wildness, which was often synonymous with being uncivilized.127

Also comparable is the use of the word “wild,” a word that Edmonia would use to

describe her life, especially in her early interviews with Lydia Maria Child and Laura

Curtis Bullard. This carried multiple meanings: “relating to undomesticated animals

and uncultivated plants or land” and “Of persons (or their attributes): Uncivilized,

savage; uncultured, rude; also, not accepting, or resisting, the constituted government;

rebellious.” Collapsed together, “wild” is a multivalent word, representing places and

people, untouched by capitalism, and a form of othering that was often used as a

justification for colonization, and later, manifest destiny.128

       In a way then, wild was synonymous with un-American, and to be truly

American was to be unaffiliated with markers that could be read as uncivilized. To be

an artist, a neoclassical artist, was to achieve the highest point of civilization. How

Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist then, could be read as, how the child of a wild

Indian and a negro became civilized. And it is Edmonia’s encounter with a statue of

Benjamin Franklin that answers this question, and thereby marks the climax of How

Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist:
