       Throughout her life, Lewis often faced unfair, and even criminal accusations.

To resist this discrimination, she would have to use her intelligence in creative ways.

Fortunately, she also had the support of many powerful people to buoy her against the

weight of her detractors. William Lloyd Garrison, Henry Ward Beecher, Wendell

Phillips, Gerrit Smith, and Horace Greeley were among the donors and fundraisers for

the school. Some of these individuals would meet and interact with Lewis to varying

degrees, all playing a positive role in the development of her career. Garrison, for

example, is a pivotal connection Lewis made when she first arrived in Boston, only a

few years after her time at New York Central College. Marilyn Richardson notes,

“Among the portrait busts which later earned her a singular reputation as a chronicler

of abolitionist stalwarts were those of Frederick Douglass, Charles Sumner, Maria

Weston Chapman, James McCune Smith, and John Brown, all friends of the

McGrawville school.”182 Even if Lewis was unaware of these individuals' connection

to McGrawville when she was a student there, she might have been made aware later.
