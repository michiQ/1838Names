  How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist and Reconstruction Era Memory Work

       Senator and abolitionist Charles Sumner donated How Edmonia Lewis Became

an Artist to Harvard University on November 1 of 1870, a little less than four years

before he died.57 The circumstances by which Sumner acquired a copy of this

pamphlet, and the value he attached to it, gives a new meaning to the relationships and

events of Edmonia Lewis’ life around that time. It also gives us a sense of who

belonged to the primary audience for How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist, and how

it fit into larger collecting and archiving efforts. There are several factors that may

have contributed to Sumner’s decision to include this small pamphlet in his donation

to Harvard. During the mid-nineteenth century, there was a widespread effort amongst

abolitionists and educational institutions to collect and preserve content related to

slavery, anti-slavery, and abolition. In his entry for Encyclopedia of African American

Culture and History, vol. 1 entitled “Books Collectors and Collections,” Black Studies

scholar Richard Newman notes that large institutions across the north and east were

accepting ephemera relating to slavery and abolition, including Harvard University.58
