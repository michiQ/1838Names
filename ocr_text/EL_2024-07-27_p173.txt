       With high expectations, the first "Convention of Colored Newspaper Men" was

held in Cincinnati in August of 1875 with Governor P.B.S. Pinchback as chair.

Cincinnati, just north of the Kentucky border, was a destination for enslaved people

seeking freedom. Accessible from the South, the Northeast, and the West regions of

the United States, Cincinnati was an ideal location for the first gathering of the Black

press. Delegates were selected to represent the newspapers from eight states, including

Kentucky, Tennessee, California, Louisiana, Texas, Pennsylvania, Ohio, and New

York. Almost all the papers that were mentioned in the New National Era’s “Pioneer

Papers” article were present. Not all the representatives for these papers were on their

editing staff. Some papers were represented by clergy or politicians, a point that

underscores the interconnections between Black Press, Black Church, and Black

political leadership. Five of the newspapers represented at this first colored press

convention had at least one article or advertisement that mentioned Edmonia Lewis.324

These included, the Weekly Louisianan, represented by P.B.S. Pinchback and G.T.

Rubey, (the politicians previously mentioned), The Elevator, a San Francisco

newspaper represented by Louis H. Douglass, the son of Frederick Douglass, The

Pacific Appeal, another California newspaper represented by M.W. Gibbs, and The

Christian Recorder, represented by Benjamin William Arnett and Henry McNeal
