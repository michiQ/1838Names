base of the sculpture arrived in May of 1876. However, in late June, it was reported

that the bust itself, commissioned to another artist, had not been started, and that

unveiling would be postponed to September 22, anniversary of the writing of the

Emancipation.340 Then, unfortunately, a report came in October that the monument

had been destroyed, “lost in the Susquehanna River.” They were able to finish a

replacement, and the unveiling was rescheduled for November.341 The entire ordeal

around erecting this monument to Richard Allen must have been frustrating, and, in

the end, disappointing. Transporting sculptures was a great risk and great cost,

considering the precariousness of shipping at the time. This was especially true for the

Black community, given the limited resources they had in circulation. They had to

fundraise through 1876, and ultimately, were not able to make the statement that they

had hoped to make with a July 4th unveiling.
