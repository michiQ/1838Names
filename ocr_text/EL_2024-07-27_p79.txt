characterized as wild and uncouth. She is unyielding to Miss Ophelia’s training, yet

eventually is reformed under the influence of little Eva. Knowing that most readers

would have understood this literary reference, she uses it to evoke the narrative of

savage to civilized to create a readily accessible personal narrative for her audience.

Topsy lived entirely outside of the domestic space, having no knowledge of her age,

parentage, or where she was from. Her famous response-- “I spect I grow’d. Don’t

think nobody never made me” --to Miss Ophelia’s queries points to a life without

child rearing, education, and love. Like the orphan boys Oliver Twist and others,

Topsy had been unprotected, and her so-called mischievous behavior served as a form

of self-protection. Little Eva is the foil of Topsy; whereas little Eva is frail, angelic

and good, Topsy is energetic, sinful, wicked.141 The visual imagery evoked by the

novel accentuate this difference. Being black, naturally evil, and enslaved, Topsy was

subject to abuse, and next to Little Eva, angelic, and the loved and protected heiress of

the family, she elicits patronizing pity rather than sympathy and adoration. The “little”
