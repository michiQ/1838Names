Edmonia Lewis was permitted to display her sculpture at this event. It goes without

saying then, that Douglass, amongst any of the other Black visitors to the centennial,

went to see Lewis’ exhibit, or at least heard about it.

       The silence of the Christian Recorder and the New National Era regarding

Edmonia Lewis after the “Convention of Colored Newspaper Men” prompts questions

about their opinion on “Death of Cleopatra.” In her essay, "The Death of Cleopatra /

the Birth of Freedom: Edmonia Lewis at the New World's Fair," Susanna Gold makes

the following statement in her discussion of nineteenth century interpretations of

Cleopatra’s race:

        . . . mid-century black intellectuals such as the abolitionist Henry
       Highland Garnet, novelist William Wells Brown, minister James W. C.
       Pennington, and physician Martin Robison Delaney [. . .] upheld an
       Afrocentric theory of Egyptian society and located the origins of its
       greatness in African peoples. Those who upheld the blackness of
       Cleopatra cited her mixed lineage, with only the thinnest thread of
       Greek heritage diluted by generations of racial intermixing.346
