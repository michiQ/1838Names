Garrison, her first benefactor in Boston.304 However, she presented the statue to

Reverend Grimes, and she modeled a bust of him before she returned to Europe.

While we do not know exactly what made Lewis decide to present the work to

Reverend Grimes instead of Garrison, what is clear is that her choice was fitting.

Grimes played a pivotal role on the Underground Railroad, and self-emancipated

persons made up a substantial portion of his congregation at Twelfth Baptist Church.

Many of the church’s congregants would resonate with the sculpture: a supplicant

woman, looking to heaven and kneeling before God, hands clasped in gratitude for

answering her prayer for freedom. Standing to her left is a man, presumably her

husband, who also looks up and ahead. His right arm rests on her right shoulder, while

his left arm is raised. Around his left wrist is a shackle, and in his left hand is the

unfastened shackle that would have been around his right hand. For the many self-

emancipated African Americans who attended Twelfth Baptist Church, this image

would be symbolic of their difficult past and their hopes for the future.
