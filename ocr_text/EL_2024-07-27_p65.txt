in Philadelphia. Lewis received great notoriety there while exhibiting her largest

sculpture, “Death of Cleopatra.” Sampson describes a conversation with Lewis:

       Some of her own people who had been more favored in point of
       opportunity, gave her no encouragement but came to criticise her work,
       of the merits of which they knew nothing; it was presumptuous; she
       had no time for them; and instead of fooling her with our people aping
       the prejudices of the whites. . . 109

Sampson then quoted her as follows: "I am going back, to Italy, to do something for

the race - something that will excite the admiration of the other races of the earth."

What Lewis was looking for was encouragement and admiration, but it seems that she

would not be able to get it from the Black people she encountered at the exhibition.

She was, as Sampson called her, “a lover of her race,” and she would continue to

receive praise and support from African Americans on several other occasions.110

Through her career, Lewis had to do a lot of maneuvering, and the contradictions of

her words across her career point to the chaotic nature of race as Ernest described.
