             Introducing Herself: Edmonia Lewis’ Visual Economics

       How Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist begins with this sentence: “Edmonia

was born in a wigwam, her mother being an Indian and her father a negro.”70 This

introduction is a visual queue with culturally significant connotations. Wigwam, a

term for a Native American dwelling, was popularized by widely read American

authors James Fenimore Cooper, Henry Rowe Schoolcraft, Henry Wadsworth

Longfellow, and Samuel G. Drake.71 For readers of How Edmonia Lewis Became an

Artist, this introductory sentence may have evoked images from literature, art, news

media, or exhibits and bazaars. Kirsten Pai Buick quotes the first words of How

Edmonia Lewis Became an Artist in chapter three of her book, “Longfellow, Lewis,

and the Cultural Work of Hiawatha,” to introduce her argument that, “Lewis was

wholly complicit in the construction of herself as ‘authentic,’ and by extension

‘other.’” To further build her argument, Buick cites an article in the San Francisco

Chronicle describing the Santa Clara Valley Fair and Races of 1873, in which the

reporter notes that Lewis preferred to call her booth a wigwam.72 Reporters continued
