While Church Terrell was born about twenty years after Lewis, her experience of

traveling as a Black girl provides insight into what Lewis may have had to deal with

while riding America’s trains. During the 1860s, when Church Terrell was traveling as

a five-year-old girl, she had the protection of her father who stood up to the conductor

who intimidated her when she he found her sitting in the ladies car. Lewis, who left

Oberlin just after Church Terrell was born, and would have just relocated to Italy by

the time Church Terrell was five years old, traveled a great distance unaccompanied,

without the protection of a male relative or friend. Did Lewis have to suffer the same

humiliation and disrespect as Mary Church Terrell? Was she too ordered, “to go back

to one of the rear seats where they belonged”?

          Prior to the Civil Rights Act of 1875, it was up to the discretion of hotel

keepers, train conductors, and other travel industry workers to decide how they would
treat Black customers or if they would serve them at all. As described above, there

were a variety of ways to travel from the east coast to the west coast, and the treatment

Lewis received depended on the route, region, and even time of day that she traveled.
