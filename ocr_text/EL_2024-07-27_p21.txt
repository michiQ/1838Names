This project will be in conversation with scholarship on Black women’s print activism

in the late nineteenth century. Martha Jones’ All Bound Up Together: The Woman

Question in African American Public Culture, 1830-1900 (2007) explores African

American women’s activism at the end of the nineteenth century, placing an emphasis

on women’s roles in public culture. Essentially, Jones thinks about the question, “who

and what could a woman be?” in the public sphere.14 While I will not spend time on

the meaning of private and public spheres directly, the subject of my project does give

an answer the question put forward by Jones. Studying Edmonia Lewis’ exhibition and

commemoration practices actualizes new possibilities for Black women’s influence in

the public sphere. Many of the events centered on her art took place in African

American churches, and in this way contributed to African American cultural practice.

This also places her in contact with black leaders and politicians. To this end, Kevin

K. Gaines’ book, Uplifting the Race Black Leadership, Politics, and Culture in the

Twentieth Century, and particularly, chapter one, “Uplift, Dissemblance, Double-

Consciousness, and the Ideological Dimensions of Class,” is pivotal to how I

contextualize Lewis’ social world.15 This chapter gave me a foundation for reading

nineteenth -entury texts by Black writers. In addition, to show the entanglement of the

visual and literary during this time, I will be using the term “post-bellum, pre-
Harlem,” created by Caroline Gebhard Barbara McCaskill. During this period, African
