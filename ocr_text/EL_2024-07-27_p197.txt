America. There was a greater urgency for political and material change, and there was

an increase in literacy and education, which led to the growth of Black scholarship. By

the 1880s, a “National Colored Press Convention” met almost every year.366 Also, two

other texts were published in 1893, aside from Wells-Barnett’s The Reason Why

included: Noted Negro Women by Monroe Alpheus Majors, and Women of Distinction

by Lawson A. Scruggs.367 And of course, the year before, Anna J. Cooper’s A Voice

from the South was published. All these texts mention Lewis. In 1894, Frederick

Douglass would compose a 65-page essay draft entitled “Slavery,” his contribution to

a 22-chapter illustrated history of African American from 1619, a project of Harvard

Publishing Company’s Historical Department that was never completed.368

       This effort to publish anti-racist discourse coincided with an impulse to

preserve and commemorate African American history. This impulse carried across

Black writers and orators from a variety of professional backgrounds: lawyers,

journalists, teachers, politicians, physicians, and of course, religious leaders. This

period of Black scholarship culminated in the formation of the American Negro

Academy in 1897. Its founder, Alexander Crummell, was an episcopal reverend, and

had been teaching at Howard University for two years before starting the academy.
